Pathways of Knowledge and Growth

Luke Morgan (2024)

These green and white lines repeated around the painting represent the struggle that Holmesglen went through during their journey to get to where they are now.

The pattern work contained by and including these light green shapes show obstacles being overcome and represents the on-going struggles and challenges of life and learning. There will always be difficulties, but we will always eventually break through and get around them.

The pathways running across the picture in a shape of a snake stand for Holmesglen's journey from where we were (came from) to where we are going (into the future).

This shape is one of the styles that represent the seven Holmesglen campuses. The layered dots within the center hint at the large numbers of different people present on campus and capture feelings of community.

The canvas background uses green and brown hues - reminiscent of the ground, the hills and the waterways of the surrounding country.

This shape is one of the styles that represent the seven Holmesglen campuses. The patterns speak to gatherings around aboriginal campsites and the stripes within the U shapes hint at individual people.

These circles within circles connected to each other by long lines represent individuals connected together in a community. They run next to the pathways indicating the connections we will make on our journey.

‘My artwork tells the story of the Holmesglen Institute and its seven Campuses as they sit on some of the surrounding lands of the Kulin Nation Woiwurrung “Wurundjeri”, Bunurong, Wathaurong, Taungurung and Dja Dja Wurrung people.

This painting also represents togetherness as we work together as one, culture as we share and respect and acknowledge First Nations people, our students as we teach them to be the best they can be, our staff, and the journey and future of Holmesglen as we work together.’

—— Luke Morgan (2024) ——

Luke never sketches ideas or plans out artwork. Instead, he lets his hand do the talking. This piece started as a demonstration of his art during ‘O’ week and NAIDOC week 2023. Once the piece was back home, Luke found employment with Holmesglen in the Koori Support Unit and decided he wanted to tell the story of Holmesglen through the artwork.

As the painting developed, Luke found himself enjoying the process more and more. Once it was finished, he had grown quite attached and it almost ended up on his wall at home. But in a RAP committee meeting in early 2024, it was suggested we include the piece as the cover art for the Holmesglen RAP and fortunately for us, Luke liked the idea!
